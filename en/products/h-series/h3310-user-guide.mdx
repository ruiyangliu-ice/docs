## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Purpose and scope

This document provides the H3310 camera specifications and design details for developers who integrate and use the product.

### 1.2 Terminology

| Baseline | In a stereo depth camera, the baseline is the horizontal distance between the two cameras. More precisely, it is the distance between their optical axes and is typically measured in millimeters (mm). |
| --- | --- |
| Depth | Like a color or monochrome video stream, a depth stream assigns each pixel a value. This value represents the pixel's distance from the camera. |
| FOV | Field of view. The angular extent of a scene that the camera can capture. It includes horizontal FOV (HFOV), vertical FOV (VFOV), and diagonal FOV (DFOV). |
| IR camera | An infrared camera. In this product, it primarily captures images in the near-infrared spectrum. |
| RGB camera | A color camera. |
| Depth camera | A camera with a depth imaging module that typically consists of a dot projector, IR camera, and depth processor. Its color imaging module is typically an RGB camera. |
| SoC | A system-on-chip that processes image data, calculates depth, and runs application algorithms. |

### 1.3 Structured-light technology

Binocular structured light is a common 3D vision measurement technology that combines stereo vision with structured-light projection. It uses two cameras and a structured-light projector to capture an object's 3D shape and depth.

The process works as follows:

1.  Structured-light projection: A projector casts structured light onto the target object. An infrared or visible-light source typically produces the light, which is specially encoded or modulated using a grid or speckle pattern. The projected light creates bright, dark, or distorted patterns on the object's surface.

2.  Stereo imaging: While the structured light is projected, two cameras capture the target object simultaneously. The cameras are separated by a fixed distance, similar to the spacing between human eyes. They record both the projected pattern and the object's appearance.

3.  Image capture: The cameras capture left and right images. Distortion or displacement of the projected pattern in these images encodes the object's depth information.

4.  Image processing: Differences between the left and right images are used to calculate surface depth. Combining these differences with the projected pattern produces a 3D point-cloud model of the object.

5.  Measurement and reconstruction: The resulting 3D point cloud can be used to measure, analyze, or reconstruct the object's shape and dimensions.

## 2. Product overview

The H3310 is an RGB-D industrial structured-light camera that weighs 400g. You can mount it on a compound robot or small robotic arm. At a recognition distance of 30-60cm, it provides high-precision 3D data and high-resolution RGB data. Its integrated computing platform runs AI application algorithms independently for industrial robot recognition, inspection, and positioning tasks.

### 2.2 Features

- Integrated computing platform: Provides 6.0 TOPS of computing power and runs application algorithms without an external CPU.

- 3D data output: Combines structured-light imaging with AI algorithms to provide high-precision 3D data.

- Compact design: Weighs 400g and provides open interfaces for deployment.

- Mass production: Supports stable, high-volume production.

| 1 | The camera does not respond when you try to open it | Check whether the firewall is disabled. You must disable the firewall. |

| 3 | Camera data is unstable after opening the camera | Use a Gigabit Ethernet cable. Data may initially be unstable with a 100 Mbps Ethernet cable. |
