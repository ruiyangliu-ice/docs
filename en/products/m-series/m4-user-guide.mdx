## 1. Product overview

This guide describes the M4 camera developed by Zhejiang MRDVS Technology Co., Ltd., including its specifications, features, and operating instructions.

The M4 product family includes the M4 Lite and M4 cameras. These ToF products use a Gigabit Ethernet communication interface and a 24VDC power supply.

### 1.1 Product features

- Built-in 1Tops compute capacity for operation without an external CPU

- RGB-D support

- Standalone obstacle avoidance, docking, localization, soft-package grasping, storage-location detection, and volume measurement

- Gigabit Ethernet port

### 1.2 Terms of use

**Observe the following requirements during use:**

- Do not remove labels from the product.

- Do not disassemble the product. Disassembly may damage it.

- Do not use the product in strong magnetic fields.

- Do not use the product under intense sunlight above 100KLUX.

- To clean the enclosure, wipe it with a soft, lint-free cloth lightly dampened with purified water or alcohol.

- Keep the original packaging and use it whenever possible during transport.

## 2. Product specifications

### 2.1 Distance measurement principle

The M4 camera uses time-of-flight (ToF) ranging. It measures the time between emitting light and receiving the reflection from an object to calculate the distance between the object and camera.

The M4 camera captures depth directly, providing high-quality depth information with minimal compute resources and building depth scenes at a high frame rate. Like LiDAR, ToF cameras can use pulsed ToF (P\_ToF) or continuous-wave modulated ToF (CW\_ToF). P\_ToF emits light pulses, usually invisible, and calculates distance from their round-trip time. CW\_ToF emits modulated continuous light and calculates distance from the phase difference between the emitted and returned light.

### 3.3 Detection range

Figure 3.4 shows the M4 detection area. The camera's horizontal field of view is 81°, and its vertical field of view is 61°. Detection distance depends on optical power. Higher optical power and target reflectivity increase the near blind zone but reduce the far blind zone. Lower optical power and target reflectivity reduce the near blind zone but increase the far blind zone. For objects with 10%~90% reflectivity, the M4 measurement range is 0.3~5m. Beyond 5m, weak reflected signals are more susceptible to ambient light and produce larger errors.

### 3.6 Optical coordinate systems and origin

**Optical coordinate systems:**

- The optical coordinate systems include the camera coordinate system (CCS) and world coordinate system (WCS).

- The camera coordinate system is the two-dimensional depth-image coordinate system. Its origin is the optical center.

- The world coordinate system is the three-dimensional point-cloud coordinate system.

- Use the camera intrinsics to convert depth-image coordinates to 3D point-cloud coordinates. See the SDK examples.

## 4. Accuracy and precision

### 4.1 Accuracy

Calibration removes fixed offsets between sensor measurements and actual values by mapping one to the other. To calibrate an M4 camera, mount it on a rail and point it at a white wall. Map each pixel's measured value to the distance between the camera and the corresponding wall area, then correct measurements with this mapping, as shown in Figure 4.1.

<Frame>
  <img src="/images/legacy/f56d3e54-eb77-4bdb-9576-65f9aeb7a628/67a4d596-b169-43c0-93b0-dfa2ef85c421.avif" alt="M4 calibration setup" />
</Frame>

Figure 4.1. Calibration

The M4 uses active ranging, so many factors affect its accuracy. Improper installation can introduce multipath effects and distort measurements.

This is especially noticeable when measuring a low-reflectivity target beside a highly reflective object. Ideally, light follows the blue path. Light reflected from a wall may also reach the target and return to the camera along the green path. Illumination reflected directly from the adjacent high-reflectivity object may enter the camera along the red path. Optical imperfections turn this light into glare that interferes with ranging, as shown in Figure 4.2.

<Frame>
  <img src="/images/legacy/be0eb189-bf98-4c78-b2dc-3a4e8a356124/04671b63-d54f-4cac-8774-4afb08cccbc2.avif" alt="M4 installation and reflection paths" />
</Frame>

Figure 4.2. Camera installation

### 4.2 Precision

Precision typically refers to single-point variation (1-σ). As an optoelectronic measurement device, the M4 is affected by ADC conversion, signal-to-noise ratio, and system noise. Figure 4.3 shows the relationship between measurement precision and signal intensity.

<Frame>
  <img src="/images/legacy/16312574-c0be-48c1-81b8-0cfc40ff06ae/74264eea-5a19-48a4-8520-84410a78262c.avif" alt="Measurement precision and signal intensity" />
</Frame>

Figure 4.3. Measurement precision and signal intensity

The M4 converts optoelectronic analog signals into digital data. During ADC conversion, the analog value is rounded to the smallest resolution step, introducing noise. A 1.1V signal may be represented as 1V, producing 10% error. A 10.1V signal may become 10V, and a 100.1V signal may become 100V, each producing about 1% error. ADC conversion therefore introduces more noise and error at low signal levels. A ToF camera's signal-to-noise ratio generally follows SNR∝1/√t, where `t` is integration time. Increasing integration time raises signal intensity.

## 5. Multi-camera interference

### 5.1 Parallel interference

When two cameras are installed in parallel, each can receive both its own reflected illumination and illumination from the other camera, interfering with depth data. Parallel interference increases measurement error when two M4 cameras face the same direction.

<Frame>
  <img src="/images/legacy/af686e8c-9957-4707-a2e4-1454fccc29b5/36fa2e7f-5002-4f7d-941c-841fa869060d.avif" alt="Parallel camera interference" />
</Frame>

To avoid parallel interference, install the cameras at an angle to the horizontal. If they must be installed at 0° to the horizontal plane, contact MRDVS for a custom configuration.

### 6.2 SDK output order

![SDK output order](/images/legacy/c475e8c8-74c3-4950-bec1-a5ba15f8d9cf/4bcb3dbe-c3e6-47a5-a08f-00691b16a489.avif) 

Figure 6.1. SDK output order

The SDK outputs one frame each of depth and amplitude data at the configured resolution. Each frame is stored as a one-dimensional array, beginning at the upper-left pixel and proceeding from left to right, then top to bottom. Figure 6.1 shows output along the x-axis followed by the y-axis, producing this order:

(x,y):(0,0)(0,1)(0,2)(0,…)(1,0)(1,1)(1,2)(1,…)(2,…)(3,…)(…,…).

Each depth-image pixel is a 16-bit unsigned short representing the distance from the measured object to the camera plane.

Each amplitude-image pixel is a 16-bit unsigned short representing the returned signal intensity.

### 6.3 IP address

The M4 camera's default IP address is 192.168.100.82. To change the IP address or enable DHCP, use the demo application provided by MRDVS.

### 6.4 Demo application

### 6.4.1 IP configuration

Connect the power cable, then connect the camera to the computer with an Ethernet cable. Because the camera's factory-default IP address is 192.168.100.82, set the computer to the 192.168.100.x subnet, excluding 100.82, with subnet mask 255.255.255.0. Open **Settings** → **Ethernet** → **Change adapter options**. Right-click **Ethernet**, select **Properties**, then select **Internet Protocol Version 4 (TCP/IPv4)**. For example, set the IP address to 192.168.100.20 and the subnet mask to 255.255.255.0.

### 6.4.2 Open the application

Download and install the software, then double-click the LxCameraViwer demo icon. The host application opens, searches for a camera, opens it, and starts acquisition automatically when one camera is connected.

**Note: On first launch, some systems require you to allow the application through the firewall.**

The application includes basic controls, image selection, camera configuration, and camera information. Camera information includes the IP address, ID, MAC address, serial number, and software and algorithm versions. Select the depth, amplitude, RGB, or point-cloud checkbox to display that view in the center panel.

Figure 6.2. Image display

### 6.4.3 Save images

Select **Save** to save the selected images to the `./data` folder in the installation directory. The terminal also prints the path, for example: `save path= ./data/<camera-ID>`.

After saving, `_a` identifies an amplitude image, `_d` a depth image, and `_r` an RGB (MONO) image. `.pcd` identifies a point cloud. Depth and amplitude images use PGM format, RGB images use PNG, and point clouds use PCD.

### 6.4.4 Change the IP address

Enter the required IP address, such as 192.168.10.199. Enter subnet mask 255.255.255.0 and gateway 192.168.10.1. The gateway must be on the same subnet as the required IP address; set its last octet to 1. Confirm the change, then power-cycle the camera.
