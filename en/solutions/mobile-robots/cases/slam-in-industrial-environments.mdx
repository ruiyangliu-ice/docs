SLAM technology, that is, "real-time positioning and map construction" technology, allows robots to position themselves and build maps at the same time in unknown environments. Specifically, SLAM uses sensors (such as depth cameras or lidar) to collect environmental data, and processes these data through algorithms to estimate the location of the device while building a map of the surrounding environment. SLAM technology can solve the problem of how a robot can draw a map without knowing its own position, and at the same time rely on the map to determine its position. This technology is widely used in fields such as robot navigation, autonomous driving, and augmented reality (AR), enabling devices to navigate complex environments without pre-defined maps.

Meyer Micro Vision’s top-view navigation SLAM technology utilizes RGB-D cameras to capture detailed depth data from the ceiling. The system is able to detect features at distances of up to 12 meters, making full use of the space above. This innovative approach allows the robot to map its environment while simultaneously tracking its position within the map. Unlike traditional SLAM that relies on ground navigation lines, MyerMicrovision's top-view navigation solution avoids many ground obstacles and provides a consistent perspective, improving positioning accuracy and reducing errors that may occur when robots collaborate.

**Application scenario one:**

A photovoltaic company has more than a dozen lines, which need to be put into production one by one. The workshop production line equipment and the mobile robot responsible for handling are gradually entering the site and put into production. That is, there are highly dynamic changes in the surrounding environment during the operation of the mobile robot. In addition, there are more than 500 robots used in the entire workshop. During operation, the traffic flow is large and the positioning accuracy of the docking machine is high. Finally, the Myer Micro Vision top-view SLAM navigation solution was used to replace the traditional 2D lidar navigation and positioning. The sensor was installed on the top of the photovoltaic integrated handling robot. It has been running stably for more than a year. The factory's two workshops total 80,000 square meters, and more than 500 AGVs are equipped with this visual positioning module. Since its launch, there have been no problems with operation failures and positioning loss.

**Application scenario 2:**

A South China automobile manufacturing company has an empty workshop environment, surrounded by turnover trucks, and mixed flow of people and vehicles. The stacking of parts in the factory changes greatly. It is a typical high-dynamic environment, and traditional 2D lidar positioning is very difficult. In order to meet the needs of this scenario, the Myer Micro Vision SLAM navigation and positioning solution was used to install the sensor on the upper end of the large roller handling AMR, which is responsible for transporting engine components and improving the stability of the robot's positioning.

**Application scenario three:**

A garment manufacturing company in South China has a typical floor-type warehouse with more than 4,000 storage locations. The spacing between storage locations is small and materials change greatly, making it impossible to use 2D laser navigation. Finally, Meyer Micro Vision’s top-view SLAM navigation solution was adopted, and the navigation module was installed on the top of the AGV forklift to transport pallets.
